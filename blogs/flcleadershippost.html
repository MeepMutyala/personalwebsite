A Reflection on Leadership/The Fall Leadership Conference!

What does it really mean to be a leader?

A Reflection on Leadership/The Fall Leadership Conference!

This past week, I’ve truly reflected on what it really means to be a leader.

It may sound simple. A leader is someone guides a group towards a goal. But breaking it down, it gets much, much more complicated.

You may lead a group, but why? What’s motivating you? How do you get past the challenges?

I answered this question while writing a short essay last weekend. The prompt I was given read as, “Describe the most rewarding and challenging aspect of being a leader in your school and community.” I could go on about why being a leader is rewarding, and it’s various challenging aspects, but unfortunately I was limited to 100 words.

For me, the most rewarding aspect of being a leader is the satisfaction of finishing a project with a group of people and celebrating the success of the project. I wrote, “To succeed and feel accomplishment with others, experience achievement after a great deal of hard work, and observe the impact you made on your community is very rewarding.” That basically sums up why I grew into a leader. I’ve always wanted to make bigger impacts and accomplish big things. So finally being able to say, “we did it!” with your team and celebrating your accomplishments, to me, is the most rewarding part of being a leader.

The most challenging aspect? You’re not always going to find yourself surrounded with people who aspire to be at the same level you do. I find that in certain situations, and I try to motivate others the best I can. But it comes down to whether or not they themselves are incentivized to complete the task. So I wasn’t ever sure how to deal with the problem, if trying to motivate them directly didn’t work. Other times they may be motivated, but may not know the path to follow to success. I learned later at the Fall Leadership Conference how to handle these situations, as I explain later in this post.

On Tuesday afternoon, I went down to Dover to meet my fellow state officers and help set up the 2017 CTSO Fall Leadership Conference at Delaware State University (DSU). We set up the booth for Delaware TSA, unpacked some neat pens, and checked out the room we were presenting in. My friend and fellow state officer Rishi and I also exchanged camera lenses and messed around. After setting up, we went to our hotel rooms and we rehearsed our presentation, even though it seemed we had many times. We discussed the topic we were presenting, a program called LEAP (Leadership, Education, Achievement, and Personal Growth.) LEAP was a program recently integrated into TSA last year, and many people were confused about it, so our officer team decided to go over it.

During our discussions, I realized that LEAP solves the challenges of leadership not only I may encounter, but anyone can. I won’t go over all the steps of LEAP, so I don’t bore you, but there are two specific ones that stood out to me, “Inspire A Shared Vision,” and “Enable Others To Act”. Inspiring a shared vision means to be able to motivate a group or team through sharing your goals with them, and inspiring them to set it as their goal too, so you all have a shared vision, and you all work hard towards making that vision a reality. Enabling others to act means to empower and encourage them, and to use teamwork skills to strengthen the others in your group to take action. These two steps showed how I can motivate and lead a team to success, and show them my vision of the path.

After a night of hard work (and listening to Rishi’s sick EDM/Alternative Spotify playlists), we woke up, got ready, and ate our breakfast muffins from the hotel. We drove back to DSU, a mile or two away, and went to the opening ceremony. All the state officer teams from the various organizations were called up, as we introduced ourselves and welcomed the 600 or so students attending the conference.

We walked up on stage, and Tyler took the microphone. “Hello Delaware TSA! The state officer team would like to welcome you to the Fall Leadership Conference! My name is Tyler Rouwhorst, serving as your State Sergeant-at-Arms.” The microphone was passed from officer to officer as each of us introduced ourselves with our name and position, and then it came to me. “Hello Delaware TSA, my name is Apu Mutyala, serving as your State Vice-President.” I hear a round of applause.

It’s a strange feeling, looking out into that crowd of students and saying those words. It wasn’t my first time though. The fact that I am impacting an organization with so many students amazes me. It really is an honor to do so.

I passed the microphone on, and then we all walked off the stage. After another round of applause following the state officers of the other organizations finishing their introductions, Connor, Tyler, Priya, and Rishi left in a hurry to set up to greet our audience when they came to our room. Skyler and I stayed to help direct the TSA students to our presentation site across the campus.

After arriving at the site, we waited as people settled in, and we presented our seminar. It was pretty successful, and we went over the 5 practices of being a leader and “Be. Know. Do.” in LEAP.

After the audience was dismissed to go to their next workshop, our team rushed to introduce the speaker at another seminar, and we attended a few others, ate lunch, and relaxed. Rishi and I took pictures of other presenters. At lunch, we talked about what we did well, what could be improved on, and concluded that our presentation was a success. Redesigning the whole presentation and changing it completely from the presentations in the past years, and successfully presenting it made us pretty proud of ourselves.

We went to the closing ceremony, cleaned up, and left the campus to get to our hotel. We got to our rooms at around 4:30. We got into more comfortable clothes, and Rishi, Tyler and I laid around one of our hotel rooms, watched some YouTube videos, and eventually drifted off to sleep from complete exhaustion. We woke up at 6:15 from Rishi’s alarm (thank gosh he set it) and got ready for dinner. After eating dinner, we hung around for a bit, worked on some homework, and the guys hit the pool.

We finally had time to relax and bond as an officer team some more. It became clear that we’ve come a long way since being elected in April. Our team now worked like a well-oiled machine, and we were extremely comfortable with each other. We learned a lot of skills since April, and it was quite visible.

We headed to our rooms, hung out a bit more, and then as we were about to head to sleep because it was really late, Tyler had a suggestion. “20 minute photo challenge!” he exclaimed. Rishi, Tyler, and I pulled out our DSLR’s and had 20 minutes to photograph, edit, and export a photo to Google Drive. Connor was our timekeeper and judge. After a few rounds, some “oohs” and “ahs”, we finally went to sleep.

The next day we checked out of the hotel, attended the opening ceremony, presented to the middle schoolers, went to workshops, and took some pictures, all yet again. We hung out with the Delaware BPA state officer team, played some ice breakers, and then we again cleaned up the conference, took some pictures, and headed to our homes from there.

All in all, it really was an amazing experience. I made many new connections and had loads of fun with the other officers. I’ve never realized how much I can learn from teaching others, and this past week I’ve reflected on my leadership skills and experiences more than ever. Improving my leadership skills has been my focus for a while and now it’s grown even stronger. It’s something I’ll continue to improve on as time goes on.

Thanks for reading,
-Apu Mutyala.
